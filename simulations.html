  3. Normal distribution

    4. Y= a+bX+ε

    Normal distribution is assumed in finite sample theory.

    Key assumptions of the regression model are:

    • Linearity: E(Y)=a+bX
    • Constant variance: Var(Y | X)=σ2
    • Normality : ε~N(0,σ2)
    • Independence: the observations are sampled independently.
    • X is independent of the error term: X ⊥ε

  4. Degrees of freedom:
  5. Student's t distribution and Fat tails

    6. The Student's t distribution has heavier tails than the normal distribution.

    When the degrees of freedom is large, the Student's distribution is approximately a normal distribution.

    When the degrees of freedom is small, extreme events are more likely to occur than in a normal distribution.

    Example: Simulate 50 observations with the error having a Student's t-distribution with degrees of freedom equal to 1.2.

    After doing the simulation, you can observe that it is likely to observe extreme obsevations, which move the estimated parameter far from the true value.

  7. Non-Gaussian distribution

    8. The normality assumption is needed for finite sample theory, but it is not necessary for large samples.

    As a result of the central limit theorem and the law of large numbers and under mild conditions of the error term:

    • The estimators converge to the true parameter.
    • Hypothesis testing and confident intervals are approximately valid for large samples.

  10. Slope of omitted variable:
  11. Omitted variable bias

    12. Omitted variable bias occurs when the model leaves one or more important factors.

    Suppose that the true model is y= α + β*x+ ζ* z +ε

    However, we estimate the model y= α + β*x +ε

    If x and z are uncorrelated, the only the estimated slope will have some biased caused by the misspecification of the model.

    If x and z are correlated, the the estimated paramter for the slope will be biased.
